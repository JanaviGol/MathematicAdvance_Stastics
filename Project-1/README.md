#### **Part A: Theory \& Definitions**





***1. Types of Data: Numerical \& Categorical***



Data is mainly divided into two types based on what it represents:



**Numerical Data:** 

This data represents quantities or numbers that can be measured or counted.



Example from Dataset: Household\_ Income (e.g., 45,000) and Age\_ of\_ Household\_ Head (e.g., 35) are numerical because we can perform math on them.



**Categorical Data:** 

This data represents groups or labels. It describes "qualities" rather than "quantities."



Example from Dataset: Education\_ Level (e.g., Graduate, High School) and Location (e.g., Urban, Rural) are categorical because they put households into specific groups.



***2. Types of Statistics: Descriptive vs. Inferential***



Statistics is divided into two parts depending on how we use the data:



**Descriptive Statistics:** 

This is used to summarize and describe the features of a specific dataset. It tells us "what is happening" right now in the data we have.



**Example:** Calculating the Mean income of your 200 households.



**Inferential Statistics:** 

This is used to take data from a small sample and make predictions or conclusions about a larger population.



**Example:** If we use your data to predict the average income of the entire city or country.



***3. What is Descriptive Statistics?***



Descriptive Statistics is the process of organizing, summarizing, and displaying data in a meaningful way. It helps us understand the basic patterns without looking at every single row of data.



It usually includes:



**Central Tendency:** Mean, Median, and Mode.



**Dispersion:** Range, Standard Deviation, and Variance.



**Visuals:** Histograms, Pie Charts, and Boxplots.





***4. Difference between Mean, Median, and Mode***



These are **"Measures of Central Tendency"** used to find the center of the data.



**Mean:** It is the mathematical average. We add all values and divide by the total number of rows.

**Key Feature:** It is sensitive to outliers (extreme high or low values).



**Median:** It is the middle value when data is sorted.

**Key Feature:** It is not affected by outliers and shows the true "middle" of the data.



**Mode:** It is the value that appears most frequently.

**Key Feature:** It is useful for categorical data to see the most common group.



***5. Difference between Range, Variance, and Standard Deviation***



These are "**Measures of Dispersion**" used to see how spread out the data is.



**Range:** It is the simplest measure. It is the difference between the Maximum and Minimum values.

**Limitation:** It only considers two values and ignores the rest of the data.



**Variance:** It measures the average squared distance of each point from the Mean.

**Limitation:** The result is in "squared units," which is hard to explain in real life.



**Standard Deviation:** It is the square root of Variance.

**Key Feature:** It is the most popular measure because it is in the same unit as the original data (e.g., Rupees or Years), making it easy to understand how much people's income typically varies from the average.





***Advanced Statistical Concepts*** 



**1. Gaussian (Normal) Distribution:**



It is a bell-shaped curve where the data is spread evenly on both sides of the center.



**Formula:** 

&#x20;           **f(x) = \[1 / (σ \* sqrt(2π))] \* e^(-1/2 \* ((x-μ)/σ)^2)**



**Key Fact:** In this distribution, the Mean, Median, and Mode are all equal.





**2. Log-Normal Distribution :**



This is a distribution where the data is not symmetrical and has a long tail on the right side.



**Application:** It is used for data that can never be negative, such as Household Income or stock prices.





**3. 3-Sigma Rule (Empirical Rule) :**



This rule tells us how much data falls near the average in a Normal Distribution:



**68%** of data falls within **1** Standard Deviation.



**95%** of data falls within **2** Standard Deviations.



**99.7%** of data falls within **3** Standard Deviations.





**4. Percentiles and Quartiles :**



**Percentiles:** These tell you the percentage of data that falls below a certain value. For example, the 90th percentile means you are higher than 90% of the data.



**Quartiles:** These divide the data into four equal parts:



**Q1 (25th):** The **lower** quarter.



**Q2 (50th):** The **Median** (middle).



**Q3 (75th):** The **upper** quarter.





**5. Five-Number Summary :**



This is a summary that uses 5 values to describe the entire dataset:



**Values: Minimum, Q1, Median, Q3, and Maximum.**



**Visual Tool:** We use a **Boxplot** to show this summary visually.





**6. Skewness and Kurtosis :**



**Skewness:** It measures if the data is leaning to one side.



**Positive Skew:** The tail is longer on the right side (common in income data).



**Negative Skew:** The tail is longer on the left side.



**Kurtosis:** It measures how "sharp" or "flat" the peak of the graph is.



*High Peak (Leptokurtic):* The graph is very thin and tall.



*Normal Peak (Mesokurtic)*: The graph is a perfect bell shape, not too tall and not too flat.



*Flat Peak (Platykurtic):* The graph is wide and short.
